🫀 C(i). Mammalian Circulatory System

The Heart: Your Body's Pump

🔍 Heart Structure

  • 4 chambers: 2 atria (upper, thin-walled) + 2 ventricles (lower, thick-walled)
  • Left ventricle: Thickest wall (pumps to entire body)
  • Septum: Divides left and right sides (prevents blood mixing)
  • Valves: Ensure one-way blood flow

The human heart showing four chambers and major blood vessels

Blood Vessels: The Highways

Vessel Structure Function Direction
Arteries Thick, elastic, muscular Carry blood AWAY from heart Heart → Organs
Veins Thin walls, valves present Carry blood TO heart Organs → Heart
Capillaries One-cell thick, narrow Exchange of materials Arteries ↔ Veins

Special Blood Vessels (VERY IMPORTANT!)

Vessel Path Special Function
Hepatic Portal Vein Intestine → Liver Carries digested food to liver FIRST for processing before general circulation
Pulmonary Artery Heart → Lungs ONLY artery carrying deoxygenated blood (CO₂ to lungs)
Pulmonary Vein Lungs → Heart ONLY vein carrying oxygenated blood (O₂ to heart)

Why Hepatic Portal Vein is Special:
Ensures liver processes nutrients first (detoxification, glucose storage as glycogen). This is called the hepatic portal system (connects two capillary beds).

🔍 Plasma Details

  • 90% water
  • 7% plasma proteins (albumin, globulins, fibrinogen)
  • 3% other solutes (glucose, amino acids, hormones, minerals, waste)

3. Lymph Composition & Functions

🔍 Lymph Features

  • Origin: Tissue fluid entering lymphatic vessels
  • Color: Pale yellow/clear
  • Cells: Lymphocytes (WBCs)
  • Special content: Fats (from lacteals in villi)
  • Functions: Returns tissue fluid to blood, transports fats, immune defense
🧠 KEY POINT:

Lymph is basically blood minus red blood cells and large proteins.

⚙️ Mechanisms of Transportation

A. Physical/Passive Mechanisms (No Energy)

Mechanism Definition Example
Diffusion Movement from high to low concentration Gas exchange in lungs (O₂ in, CO₂ out)
Osmosis Water diffusion across semi-permeable membrane Water absorption in roots
Plasmolysis Cell membrane shrinks from wall when water leaves Plant cells in salt solution (wilting)
Turgidity Cell swells when water enters Plant cells in pure water (firm stems)

✅ Turgid Cell
  • Cell full of water
  • Firm, upright
  • Membrane against wall
  • Good for plant
❌ Plasmolysed Cell
  • Cell lost water
  • Flaccid, wilted
  • Membrane pulled away
  • Bad for plant

B. Biological Mechanisms (Energy Required)

🔍 Active Transport

  • Definition: Movement against concentration gradient (low → high) using energy
  • Example: Root hair cells absorbing mineral ions from soil (soil has fewer minerals than root)

🔄 Circulatory System Types in Animals

🔓 Open System
  • Blood flows freely in body cavity
  • No capillaries
  • Heart → spaces → slow return
  • Simple, low pressure
  • Slow, inefficient
  • Examples: Insects, Crustaceans
🔒 Closed System
  • Blood stays in vessels always
  • Heart → Arteries → Capillaries → Veins
  • Fast, efficient
  • Can direct to specific organs
  • Complex, high energy cost
  • Examples: Mammals, Fish
🧠 MEMORY AID:

Open = Out in the open (body cavity) | Closed = Contained in vessels

🌱 Mechanisms in Plants

1. Transpiration Pull (Cohesion-Tension Theory)

Transpiration pull mechanism showing water movement from roots to leaves

How it works:

  1. Water evaporates from leaf mesophyll cells (transpiration)
  2. Creates negative pressure (tension) in xylem
  3. Water molecules are cohesive (stick together) → pull entire column up
  4. Water molecules are adhesive (stick to xylem walls) → prevent breaking
  5. Result: Continuous stream from roots to leaves

Conditions Increasing Transpiration:

  • High light intensity (stomata open)
  • Low humidity
  • High temperature
  • Wind

2. Root Pressure

  • Active transport of minerals into xylem creates osmotic gradient
  • Water follows by osmosis into xylem
  • Pushes water up (especially at night when transpiration is low)
  • Causes guttation (water droplets on leaf edges in morning)

Transpiration Pull vs. Root Pressure

Feature Transpiration Pull Root Pressure
Force Suction from top (leaves) Push from bottom (roots)
Strength Strong (100m+) Weak (few meters)
When active Daytime Nighttime
Main use Tall trees Small plants/night

3. Active Transport in Plants

Root hair cells use energy to absorb mineral ions against concentration gradient. Essential because soil water has very low mineral concentration.

⚠️ CRITICAL JAMB EXAM TIPS

  • Special Vessels: Hepatic portal (intestine→liver), Pulmonary artery (deoxygenated), Pulmonary vein (oxygenated)
  • Xylem vs Phloem: Xylem = water UP (dead cells), Phloem = food BOTH ways (living cells)
  • Blood Components: Plasma (55%), RBC (41%), WBC (4%), Platelets (<1%)
  • Lymph: Blood minus RBCs and large proteins
  • Mechanisms: Diffusion (high→low), Osmosis (water), Active transport (low→high, needs energy)
  • Transpiration Pull: Main mechanism for tall trees (cohesion-tension)
  • Root Pressure: Causes guttation, weak force
  • Open vs Closed: Open = no vessels (insects), Closed = vessels always (mammals)

🎯 Likely JAMB Questions & Answers

Q1: Which blood vessel carries deoxygenated blood away from the heart?

A: Pulmonary artery (the exception to the rule!)

Q2: Why does the hepatic portal vein carry blood from intestine to liver first?

A: To allow the liver to process nutrients (store glucose, detoxify) before general circulation.

Q3: What is the main mechanism of water transport in tall trees?

A: Transpiration pull (cohesion-tension theory)

Q4: How does active transport differ from osmosis?

A: Active transport moves against gradient using energy; osmosis is passive water movement down gradient.

Q5: What causes guttation in plants?

A: Root pressure at night when transpiration is low.
