Heredity (JAMB 2026) 🚀

Complete Notes – Modern Biology & Syllabus

Definition of Heredity

Heredity is the transmission of genetic characteristics from parents to offspring through genes, ensuring similarity between generations while allowing variation.

1. Inheritance of Characters

Heritable Characters — Genetic traits passed via genes from parents to offspring.

  • Examples: Eye colour, skin colour, blood groups (A, B, AB, O), height, attached/detached earlobes, sickle-cell trait.

Non-heritable Characters — Acquired during lifetime; not encoded in DNA.

  • Examples: Scars, muscle from exercise, tattoos, learned skills, sunburn.

2. Chromosomes – Basis of Heredity

Chromosomes: Thread-like DNA + protein structures in nucleus (humans: 46 chromosomes = 23 pairs).

Gene: Segment of DNA that codes for a specific trait/protein (basic unit of heredity).

Allele: Different forms/variants of the same gene (e.g., T = tall dominant, t = short recessive).

Transmission:

  • Meiosis: Segregation of alleles → each gamete gets one allele (haploid).
  • Fertilisation: Random recombination of alleles → genetic variation in offspring.

3. DNA Structure

Double helix (twisted ladder shape).

  • Sides: Sugar-phosphate backbone.
  • Rungs: Nitrogenous bases held by hydrogen bonds.

Base Pairing Rules (DNA):

Base Pairs With Hydrogen Bonds
Adenine (A) Thymine (T) 2
Guanine (G) Cytosine (C) 3

Purine (A, G – larger) always pairs with pyrimidine (T, C – smaller).

4. RNA Base Pairing

RNA uses Uracil (U) instead of Thymine (T).

Rules: A pairs with U (2 bonds); G pairs with C (3 bonds).

Used in transcription: DNA template strand → mRNA (A→U, T→A, C→G, G→C).

5. Mendel's Pea Plant Experiments

Studied 7 contrasting traits in true-breeding pea plants.

Advantages: Easy to grow, self-pollinate, distinct traits, many offspring.

Mendel's 7 Traits (Dominant vs Recessive):

Trait Dominant Recessive
Seed ShapeRound (R)Wrinkled (r)
Seed ColorYellow (Y)Green (y)
Pod ShapeSmooth (S)Constricted (s)
Pod ColorGreen (G)Yellow (g)
Flower ColorPurple (P)White (p)
Flower PositionAxial (A)Terminal (a)
Plant HeightTall (T)Short (t)

Laws:

  • Dominance: Dominant allele masks recessive in heterozygote.
  • Segregation: Alleles separate during gamete formation.
  • Independent Assortment: Genes for different traits assort independently (dihybrid).

Monohybrid Cross (e.g., Tall T dominant × short t recessive):

P: TT × tt → F1: All Tt (tall).

F1 × F1 → F2: 3 tall : 1 short (phenotypic); 1 TT : 2 Tt : 1 tt (genotypic).

Dihybrid Cross (e.g., Round R/Y dominant × wrinkled r/y recessive):

P: RRYY × rryy → F1: All RrYy (round yellow).

F1 × F1 → F2: 9 round yellow : 3 round green : 3 wrinkled yellow : 1 wrinkled green (9:3:3:1).

6. Homozygous vs Heterozygous

Homozygous: Two identical alleles (AA or aa) → true-breeding.

Heterozygous: Two different alleles (Aa) → hybrid/carrier; shows dominant trait.

7. Sex-Linked (X-Linked Recessive) Inheritance

Traits on X chromosome; more males affected (XY).

No father-to-son transmission.

  • Examples: Haemophilia, red-green colour blindness, male pattern baldness (sometimes listed).

Carrier female (XN Xn) × normal male (XN Y) → 50% sons affected.

8. Autosomal Recessive Inheritance (Including Sickle-Cell)

Needs two recessive alleles (aa) to express.

Carriers (Aa) unaffected.

Skips generations; equal males/females.

  • Examples: Sickle-cell anaemia (SS), cystic fibrosis, albinism.

Sickle-Cell Genetics (HbS gene):

  • AA: Normal haemoglobin, no sickle-cell disease.
  • AS: Sickle-cell trait (carrier) – mild symptoms, resistant to malaria.
  • SS: Sickle-cell anaemia – painful crises, anemia, organ damage.

Crossing AS × AS (Carrier × Carrier):

Parent 1 \ Parent 2 A S
A AA (25% – normal) AS (25% – carrier)
S AS (25% – carrier) SS (25% – sickle-cell anaemia)

Offspring: 25% AA (normal), 50% AS (carrier, malaria resistant), 25% SS (affected).

Genetic Counselling: AS × AS parents have 25% chance of SS child – advise on risks, prenatal testing.

9. Autosomal Dominant Inheritance

Needs only one dominant allele (Aa or AA) to express.

Appears every generation; male-to-male possible.

  • Examples: Huntington's disease, Marfan syndrome, neurofibromatosis, achondroplasia.

Affected (Aa) × unaffected (aa) → 50% affected offspring.

10. Applications

Agriculture: Cross-breeding (hybrids), in-breeding (uniformity but depression), out-breeding (vigour), GMOs (pros/cons: yield vs biosafety).

Medicine: Genetic counselling (sickle-cell AS × AS = 25% SS risk; Rhesus/blood groups), recombinant DNA (insulin production), gene therapy.

Quick Revision Checklist

  • Ratios: Monohybrid 3:1; Dihybrid 9:3:3:1; Sickle-cell AS × AS = 1:2:1 (AA:AS:SS).
  • Bonds: A-T/U = 2; G-C = 3.
  • Patterns: Autosomal recessive (skips, carriers); Dominant (every generation); X-linked (more males, no father-son).
