🌿 A. Internal Structure of Flowering Plants

Overview: You must identify transverse sections (TS) of root, stem, and leaf, relate structures to functions, and understand supporting tissues (collenchyma, sclerenchyma, xylem, phloem fibers).

1. ROOT (Transverse Section)

Dicot root cross-section showing vascular cylinder and tissues

Microscopic view of root showing xylem, phloem, and endodermis

🔍 Key Structure (Outside → Inside)

  • Epidermis: Single layer, no cuticle, has root hairs for absorption
  • Cortex: Parenchyma cells for storage and water passage
  • Endodermis: Barrel-shaped cells with Casparian strips (waxy suberin) - controls entry
  • Pericycle: Produces lateral roots
  • Vascular Bundle: Xylem + Phloem in radial arrangement (transport)
  • Pith: Center storage (small in dicots, large in monocots)
Feature Dicot Root Monocot Root
Xylem bundles 2-4 (diarch/tetrarch) Many (polyarch)
Pith Small or absent Large and prominent
Cambium Present (secondary growth) Absent

2. STEM (Transverse Section)

Dicot stem showing vascular bundles arranged in a ring

Comparison: Dicot (ring bundles) vs Monocot (scattered bundles)

Detailed comparison showing bundle sheath in monocot

🌲 Dicot Stem Structure

  • Epidermis: Single layer with cuticle and trichomes (protection)
  • Hypodermis: Collenchyma cells (flexible support)
  • Cortex: Parenchyma + chlorenchyma (photosynthesis, storage)
  • Endodermis: Starch sheath (nutrient storage)
  • Vascular Bundles: Arranged in a ring, collateral and open (with cambium)
  • Pith: Large central pith + medullary rays (storage)
🌳 DICOT STEM
  • Vascular bundles in ring
  • Cambium present
  • Distinct pith
  • Secondary growth
  • No bundle sheath
🌾 MONOCOT STEM
  • Vascular bundles scattered
  • No cambium
  • No distinct pith
  • No secondary growth
  • Sclerenchymatous bundle sheath
🧠 MEMORY TRICK:

"Dicot = Ring around the rosy" (bundles in ring) | "Monocot = Messy scatter" (bundles scattered)

3. LEAF (Transverse Section)

3D view of leaf cross-section showing mesophyll layers

Detailed leaf anatomy showing palisade and spongy mesophyll

🍃 Leaf Structure

  • Upper Epidermis: Covered by cuticle (prevents water loss)
  • Palisade Mesophyll: Elongated cells, many chloroplasts (main photosynthesis site)
  • Spongy Mesophyll: Round cells with large air spaces (gas exchange)
  • Lower Epidermis: Contains stomata with guard cells (gas exchange)
  • Vascular Bundle (Vein): Xylem (upper), Phloem (lower) + bundle sheath
Layer Structure Function
Cuticle Waxy, transparent Prevents water loss
Palisade cells Tightly packed, vertical Maximum light absorption
Spongy mesophyll Loose, air spaces Gas circulation (CO2/O2)
Stomata Pores with guard cells Transpiration & gas exchange

Three basic plant tissues: Parenchyma, Collenchyma, Sclerenchyma

Tissue Cell State Wall Characteristics Location Function
Collenchyma Living Unevenly thickened primary walls (pectin/cellulose) Below epidermis in stems & leaf margins Flexible support in growing regions
Sclerenchyma Dead at maturity Lignified secondary walls Seed coats, fruit cores, vascular bundles Rigid support, protection
Xylem Fibers Dead Thick lignified walls Xylem tissue Mechanical support + water transport

⚠️ CRITICAL JAMB EXAM TIPS

  • Root TS: Know epidermis, cortex, endodermis (Casparian strips), pericycle, vascular cylinder
  • Stem TS: Dicot = ring bundles, Monocot = scattered bundles (most common exam question!)
  • Leaf TS: Palisade mesophyll (photosynthesis) vs Spongy mesophyll (gas exchange)
  • Supporting tissues: Collenchyma (flexible, living) vs Sclerenchyma (rigid, dead, lignified)
  • Xylem vs Phloem: Xylem = dead, lignified, upward; Phloem = living, bidirectional
  • Mammal organs: Liver (right), Stomach (left), Kidneys (dorsal/retroperitoneal)
  • Practice drawing: JAMB often asks you to draw and label TS of root, stem, or leaf
  • Structure-function: Always relate structure to function (e.g., root hairs = absorption)

  • Phloem: Food transport
Mammal Organs

  • Stomach: Protein digestion
  • Kidney: Filtration
  • Small intestine: Absorption
