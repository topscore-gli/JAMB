– JAMB Topic: Evolution

Definition of Evolution

Organic evolution is the sum total of all adaptive changes that have taken place over a long period of time, resulting in the diversity of forms, structures, and functions among organisms. It is the gradual process by which populations of organisms change over generations through descent with modification from common ancestors, leading to new species.

Step 1: Theories of Evolution

Lamarck’s Theory (Inheritance of Acquired Characteristics)

  • Use and disuse: Organs used a lot develop; unused ones shrink.
  • Acquired traits passed to offspring.
  • Example: Giraffes stretched necks to reach leaves → longer necks inherited.
  • Rejected today (acquired traits not genetic).
  • Contribution: First to propose species change over time.

Darwin’s Theory (Natural Selection / Survival of the Fittest)

  • Overproduction → struggle for existence.
  • Variation exists naturally.
  • Fittest (best-adapted) survive and reproduce → favorable traits passed on.
  • Example: Darwin's finches — different beak shapes for different foods.

Modern Synthetic Theory (Neo-Darwinism / Organic Theory)

  • Combines Darwin + genetics.
  • Variations from mutation + recombination.
  • Natural selection acts on genetic variations.
  • Evolution = change in gene frequencies over generations.
  • Mutation provides raw material.

Step 2: Evidence of Evolution

  • Fossil Records: Show progression from simple to complex organisms over time.
  • Comparative Anatomy: Homologous structures (same basic plan, different functions).
  • Example: Forelimbs of human, cat, whale, bat.
  • Physiology: Similar biochemical processes/enzymes across species.
  • Embryology: Early embryos similar (e.g., gill slits in vertebrates).
  • Metamorphosis: Larval stages often resemble ancestral forms.

Step 3: Metamorphosis as Evidence (Embryological / Developmental)

Definition of Metamorphosis

Metamorphosis is a biological process involving abrupt or gradual physical transformation in an organism after birth or hatching, typically during the transition from juvenile to adult form. It often involves distinct stages (e.g., larva → pupa → adult) and is common in insects and amphibians. In evolutionary biology, it serves as evidence of common ancestry because early stages often resemble ancestral forms.

Toad (Incomplete / Gradual Metamorphosis)

  • Stages: Egg → Tadpole (gills, tail, aquatic) → Toadlet (legs develop, tail shortens) → Adult (lungs, land).
  • Link: Tadpole recalls fish-like ancestors.

Butterfly (Complete / Holometabolous)

  • Stages: Egg → Larva (caterpillar) → Pupa (chrysalis) → Adult (winged).
  • Link: Larva resembles primitive worm-like ancestors; allows niche separation.

Housefly (Complete)

  • Stages: Egg → Larva (maggot) → Pupa → Adult (winged fly).
  • Link: Maggot resembles ancestral form; fast cycle for rapid reproduction.

Cockroach (Incomplete / Hemimetabolous)

  • Stages: Egg (ootheca) → Nymph (mini-adult, wingless, molts several times) → Adult (wings).
  • Link: Gradual change; conserved primitive insect trait.

Step 4: Evolutionary Trends

Plants: Algae → Mosses (no vessels) → Ferns (vascular, spores) → Gymnosperms (seeds) → Angiosperms (flowers/fruits) — increasing land adaptation.

Animals: Invertebrates (simple) → Vertebrates: Fish → Amphibians → Reptiles → Birds/Mammals — from water to land, better mobility/brain/parental care.

Quick JAMB Tips

  • Memorize: Lamarck (acquired traits), Darwin (natural selection), Modern (mutation + selection).
  • Evidences: Fossil, Homologous anatomy, Embryology (metamorphosis shows ancestral stages).
  • Practice: Draw cycles + label stages; explain one evidence/example per theory.
  • Key phrase: "Sum total of adaptive changes over long time → diversity."
