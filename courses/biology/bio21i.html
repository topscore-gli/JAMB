🧬 Chapter 21: Heredity (JAMB)

DNA + chromosomes + meiosis + Punnett squares + applications (Nigeria-friendly examples)

1️⃣ What is Heredity?

Heredity is the passing of traits (characters) from parents to children.

Children often resemble parents because traits are inherited through genes

a) Inheritance of characters

(i) Heritable characters

Definition: Traits controlled by genes/DNA and can be passed to children.

  • Skin colour
  • Eye colour
  • Blood group
  • Tongue rolling
  • Sickle-cell trait

(ii) Non-heritable characters

Definition: Traits caused by environment or injury; not controlled by genes and cannot be inherited.

  • Scars from accidents
  • Muscles from gym
  • Pierced ears
  • Tattoos
  • Knowledge learned in school

✅ Quick Test (JAMB style)

  • Which is heritable? (a) height from good food (b) height because dad is tall → Answer: (b)

2️⃣ Chromosomes – Basis of Heredity

Chromosomes are thread-like structures in the nucleus made of DNA coiled around proteins.

Chromosome: DNA tightly coiled and packaged

b) (i) Structure

DNA (Deoxyribonucleic acid) is the “blueprint of life”. It carries genetic information.

DNA: double helix with sugar-phosphate backbone and paired bases

Key parts of DNA (easy)

  • Double helix = twisted ladder
  • Sides = sugar + phosphate
  • Steps = bases: A, T, C, G
  • Base pairing: A pairs with T; C pairs with G
🧠 Memory Trick:

"A-T" and "C-G" always pair (like best friends).

Gene

Definition (Gene): A gene is a small section of DNA that codes for one trait (character).

DNA coils to form chromatin, and chromatin condenses into chromosomes

🔁 Transmission of Hereditary Characters

Definition: Traits move from parents to children through genes carried in gametes (sperm and egg).

Meiosis produces gametes with half the chromosome number

Simple flow: Meiosis → gametes (one copy) → fertilization → zygote (two copies)

Objectives (JAMB)

  • Segregation: alleles separate during gamete formation (meiosis)
  • Random fertilization: sperm and egg combine randomly → siblings differ

Fertilization restores full chromosome number in the zygote

🧩 Punnett Square (Cross-breeding Data)

Definition: A Punnett square is a simple table used to predict the possible genotypes and phenotypes of offspring.

Punnett squares help predict offspring ratios

Monohybrid Cross (Tt × Tt)

Example: Tall (T) is dominant over short (t).

Result: 3 Tall : 1 Short (75% tall, 25% short)

🔑 JAMB Tip

  • Always draw a Punnett square when asked about inheritance.

Dihybrid (Quick recall)

  • Typical ratio (when independent assortment occurs): 9 : 3 : 3 : 1

🎲 Probability & Sex Determination

Probability means chance.

Key ratios

  • Monohybrid: 3:1
  • Dihybrid: 9:3:3:1

Sex determination

Key idea: Mother always contributes X. Father contributes X or Y, so the father determines the sex.

XY system: XX = female, XY = male

Chance: 50% girl (XX), 50% boy (XY)

🧠 Applications of Heredity

i) Agriculture (cross-breeding)

Cross-breeding can produce higher-yield and disease-resistant varieties

Out-breeding
  • Meaning: crossing different varieties
  • Advantage: hybrid vigour
  • Disadvantage: takes time; may be less uniform
In-breeding
  • Meaning: mating within same family line
  • Advantage: pure line, uniform traits
  • Disadvantage: weak offspring; more genetic diseases

ii) Medicine (GMO, gene therapy, counseling)

Genetic engineering: inserting a gene into another organism

Key terms

  • GMO: genetically modified organism (e.g., Bt crops)
  • Gene therapy: correcting faulty genes (developing field)
  • Biosafety: strict testing to protect humans and environment

Marriage counselling (Nigeria-relevant)

  • Rhesus factor: Rh- mother + Rh+ father can cause pregnancy issues (anti-D injection helps)
  • Sickle cell: AS × AS → 25% SS (sickle cell disease)
  • Safer combos: AA × AA, AA × AS (depends on counselling)

iii) Recombinant DNA (making medicines)

Bacteria can be engineered to produce human insulin

Examples

  • Insulin (diabetes)
  • Interferon (viral diseases)
  • Useful enzymes for medicine

iv) Sex-linked characters

Definition: Traits controlled by genes on the X chromosome (males have one X, so they show the trait more often).

X-linked inheritance pattern (males more affected)

Examples

  • Colour blindness
  • Haemophilia
  • Pattern baldness (often taught as sex-influenced/sex-linked in basic biology)

Rule (Easy)

  • Affected father → daughters can carry the allele (sons may be safe if mother normal)
  • Carrier mother → 50% sons affected (in many X-linked recessive cases)
