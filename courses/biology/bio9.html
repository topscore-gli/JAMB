🌱Part 1: Plant Support & Movement

Need for Support

  • Maintain upright position against gravity
  • Exposure to sunlight for photosynthesis
  • Transport of water and nutrients
  • Position reproductive structures for pollination

A. Tropic Movements (Tropisms) DIRECTIONAL

Phototropism: Auxin accumulates on shaded side causing cell elongation

Type Stimulus Response Example
Phototropism Light Shoots toward (+), roots away (-) Sunflower tracking sun
Geotropism Gravity Roots down (+), shoots up (-) Root penetration into soil
Hydrotropism Water Roots toward water Roots growing toward moist soil
Thigmotropism Touch Tendrils coil around supports Grapevine climbing
Chemotropism Chemicals Growth toward chemicals Pollen tube toward ovule

Auxin Mechanism:

  • Produced in shoot tips and young leaves
  • Moves away from light source
  • In stems: promotes cell elongation → bending toward light
  • In roots: inhibits cell elongation → bending downward
🧠 MEMORY TRICK:

"Shoots go to Light, Roots go to Water and Down" — Remember: Shoots (+) phototropism, Roots (+) geotropism & hydrotropism

B. Nastic Movements (Nasties) NON-DIRECTIONAL

Mimosa pudica: Thigmonasty (touch-induced folding)

Tropic vs Nastic: Directional growth vs Turgor changes

Type Stimulus Example Mechanism
Thigmonasty Touch/Shock Mimosa pudica leaf folding Turgor pressure changes
Photonasty Light intensity Flowers opening/closing Turgor changes
Thermonasty Temperature Tulip flowers in warmth Turgor changes
Nyctinasty Day-night cycle Legume leaves folding at night Circadian rhythm

Mechanism: Motor cells in pulvini pump K⁺ ions out → Water exits by osmosis → Cells lose turgor → Organ moves. Reversible when cells rehydrate!

Tropic vs Nastic Comparison

🌿 Tropic
  • Directional
  • Slow (hours-days)
  • Cell growth
  • Irreversible
  • Auxin involved
⚡ Nastic
  • Non-directional
  • Fast (seconds)
  • Turgor pressure
  • Reversible
  • No auxin

🌿Supporting Tissues in Plants

Plant tissues: Collenchyma, Sclerenchyma, Xylem, Phloem

A. Collenchyma FLEXIBLE

Collenchyma: Unevenly thickened primary walls at corners

🔍 Key Features

  • Cell wall: Primary walls unevenly thickened with cellulose/pectin at corners
  • Lignin: ABSENT
  • Living cells: YES
  • Location: Beneath epidermis in young stems, petioles, leaf veins
  • Function: Flexible support for growing organs; allows elongation

B. Sclerenchyma RIGID

Sclerenchyma: Thick, lignified secondary walls

Sclerenchyma fibers and sclereids (stone cells)

🔍 Key Features

  • Cell wall: Thick, heavily lignified secondary walls
  • Lignin: PRESENT
  • Living cells: NO (dead at maturity)
  • Types: Fibers (long) and Sclereids (short/stone cells)
  • Location: Mature stems, roots, leaf veins, seed coats
  • Function: Rigid support and protection
🧠 MEMORY TRICK:

"Coll-enchyma = Collecting (growing)" — Found in growing regions, flexible

"Scler-enchyma = Scary (hard)" — Hard, dead, lignified, permanent support

C. Xylem & Phloem

Dicot stem (ring arrangement) vs Monocot stem (scattered bundles)

Feature Xylem Phloem
Components Tracheids, vessels, fibers, parenchyma Sieve tubes, companion cells, fibers, parenchyma
Cell walls Lignified secondary walls Primary walls; fibers may be lignified
Living cells Mostly dead at maturity Yes (except fibers)
Primary function Water and mineral transport Organic nutrient transport
Secondary function Mechanical support Support (via fibers)

🦴Part 2: Animal Support & Movement

Need for Support

  • Maintain body shape against gravity
  • Support body weight (terrestrial)
  • Protection of soft internal organs
  • Locomotion — movement from place to place
  • Respiratory function — breathing muscle attachment

A. Exoskeleton EXTERNAL

Chitin exoskeleton: Epicucicle, Exocuticle, Procuticle layers

Feature Description
Location External
Composition Chitin (polysaccharide); CaCO₃ in crustaceans
Secreted by Epidermal cells
Growth Does NOT grow → requires molting (ecdysis)

Functions

  • Protection against predators
  • Support — maintains body shape
  • Prevents desiccation (water loss)
  • Muscle attachment
  • Sensory function

Disadvantages: Restricts growth; must molt; limits body size; heavy

B. Endoskeleton INTERNAL

Bone structure: Compact and spongy bone tissue

Feature Description
Location Internal
Composition Bone (calcium phosphate + collagen) and Cartilage
Growth Grows with animal
Living tissue Cellular (living cells present)

  • Continuous growth
  • Greater flexibility
  • Larger body sizes possible
  • Better repair capabilities

🦴Special Vertebrae: Atlas (C1) & Axis (C2)

Atlas (C1) and Axis (C2) showing articulation and dens

Comparison: Atlas holds the head, Axis allows rotation

⭐ ATLAS (C1) — "The Ring"

  • NO BODY: Lacks vertebral body (unique!)
  • NO SPINOUS PROCESS: Only posterior tubercle
  • RING STRUCTURE: Anterior + Posterior arches + Lateral masses
  • SUPERIOR ARTICULAR FACETS: Large, concave, kidney-shaped (articulate with skull)
  • Function: Supports skull; nods head ("Yes")

⭐ AXIS (C2) — "The Pivot"

  • ODONTOID PROCESS (DENS): Tooth-like projection upward — MOST DISTINCTIVE FEATURE
  • BODY: Present below dens
  • SUPERIOR ARTICULAR FACETS: Large, flat, slightly convex (articulate with Atlas)
  • Function: Allows head rotation ("No" movement)

Atlas vs Axis Comparison

Feature ATLAS (C1) AXIS (C2)
Vertebral Body ❌ ABSENT ✅ Present (below dens)
Spinous Process ❌ Absent (tubercle only) ✅ Large, bifid
Dens/Odontoid ❌ Absent ✅ PRESENT
Overall Shape Ring-like Typical + upward projection
Primary Movement Flexion/Extension (Yes) Rotation (No)

🧠 MEMORY TRICKS:

"Atlas holds the world" → Ring-shaped, supports skull, no body to get in the way

"Axis is the pivot" → Has the tooth-like dens that Atlas rotates around

"Yes = Atlas" (nodding), "No = Axis" (shaking head side to side)

Clinical Note: C1 and C2 differ because C1's body fuses with C2 during development to become the dens. Fractures here are serious due to proximity to brainstem.

⚠️ CRITICAL JAMB EXAM TIPS

  • Root responses: Positive geotropism (down), Negative phototropism (away from light)
  • Auxin effects: Promotes stem growth, INHIBITS root growth
  • Exoskeletons: NON-LIVING; endoskeletons are LIVING
  • Atlas (C1): NO body, NO spinous process — ring-shaped
  • Axis (C2): Has the dens (odontoid process) for rotation
  • All cervical vertebrae: Have transverse foramen (not just C1/C2)
  • Thoracic vertebrae: Only ones with rib facets
  • Collenchyma: Living, flexible, growing regions
  • Sclerenchyma: Dead, rigid, lignified, mature regions
  • Nastic movements: Turgor pressure (fast, reversible), NOT growth

📋Quick Revision Checklist

🌿 Plants
  • Phototropism: Light → Auxin → Bend
  • Geotropism: Roots (+), Shoots (-)
  • Collenchyma: Living, flexible
  • Sclerenchyma: Dead, lignified
  • Nastic: Turgor (fast)
🦴 Animals
  • Exoskeleton: Chitin, molt
  • Endoskeleton: Bone, grows
  • Atlas: No body, ring
  • Axis: Has dens, pivot
  • Cervical: Transverse foramen

Key Distinguishing Features

  • Tropic: Directional, growth-based, slow, irreversible
  • Nastic: Non-directional, turgor-based, fast, reversible
  • Atlas (C1): No body, no spine, ring-shaped
  • Axis (C2): Has dens, allows rotation
  • Hinge joint: One plane (elbow, knee)
  • Ball & socket: All planes (shoulder, hip)
