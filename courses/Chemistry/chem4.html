1️⃣ Concepts: Atom, Molecule & Ion

Atom: the smallest particle of an element that can take part in a chemical reaction. It is neutral, with a nucleus (protons + neutrons) and electrons around it.

Molecule: two or more atoms bonded together (usually neutral), e.g., H₂, CO₂.

Ion: a charged atom or molecule formed by gaining or losing electrons.

Basic atom: nucleus (p⁺, n⁰) with electrons (e⁻) around it

Ion types

  • Cation (+): formed by losing electrons (e.g., Na → Na⁺ + e⁻)
  • Anion (-): formed by gaining electrons (e.g., Cl + e⁻ → Cl⁻)
🧠 Quick distinction

Atom = neutral single particle • Molecule = neutral group • Ion = charged particle

2️⃣ Historical Development (Key Scientists)

Atomic models changed with new experiments (Dalton → Thomson → Rutherford → Bohr)

Who did what (JAMB loves this)

  • Dalton (1808): atoms are tiny solid spheres; combine in fixed ratios
  • Thomson (1897): discovered electron; “plum pudding” model
  • Rutherford (1911): small dense nucleus; atom mostly empty space
  • Millikan (1909): measured charge of electron (1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C)
  • Moseley (1913): atomic number = number of protons (periodic table order)
  • Bohr (1913): electrons in fixed energy levels; hydrogen spectrum explained
🧠 Quick chain

Thomson found e⁻ → Rutherford found nucleus → Bohr arranged e⁻ in shells.

🧪 Rutherford Gold Foil Experiment

Key result: Most alpha particles passed straight through (so atom is mostly empty space). A few were deflected strongly (so nucleus is small, dense, and positively charged).

Gold foil experiment: strong deflections show a dense positive nucleus

✅ What it disproved

  • It disproved Thomson’s plum pudding model (positive charge is not spread everywhere).

3️⃣ Atomic Structure, Numbers & Isotopes

Atomic number (Z): number of protons (and electrons in a neutral atom).

Mass number (A): protons + neutrons.

Neutrons: n⁰ = A − Z

Isotopes

Isotopes: atoms of the same element with the same Z but different A (different neutrons).

Isotopes: same protons, different neutrons (example: hydrogen)

Examples

  • Hydrogen: ¹H, ²H
  • Carbon: ¹²C, ¹³C, ¹⁴C
  • Chlorine: ³⁵Cl, ³⁷Cl (gives RAM ≈ 35.5)

Average atomic mass (weighted average)

Formula: RAM = (abundance₁×mass₁ + abundance₂×mass₂)/100

Example idea: Cl is a mixture of ³⁵Cl and ³⁷Cl → RAM becomes ~35.5

4️⃣ Quantum Numbers, Orbitals & Electron Configuration

Quantum numbers describe where an electron is likely to be found and its energy.

Aufbau order: electrons fill lowest energy orbitals first

Rules for filling electrons

  • Aufbau: fill lowest energy first (1s, 2s, 2p, 3s, 3p, 4s, 3d…)
  • Pauli: max 2 electrons per orbital with opposite spins
  • Hund: fill orbitals singly before pairing in a subshell

Elements 1–20 (must practice)

  • Na (11): 1s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s¹
  • Ca (20): 1s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s² 3p⁶ 4s²

🟢 Shapes of s and p Orbitals

s orbital: spherical; holds 2 electrons.

p orbitals: dumbbell-shaped; 3 orientations (px, py, pz); total 6 electrons for p subshell.

Orbital shapes: s (sphere), p (dumbbell in 3 directions)

📈 Periodic Table & Periodicity

The periodic table is arranged by increasing atomic number. Rows are periods, columns are groups.

Periodic table: position helps predict properties

Families (know examples)

  • Group 1: alkali metals (e.g., Na) — very reactive
  • Group 2: alkaline earth metals (e.g., Ca)
  • Group 17: halogens (e.g., Cl)
  • Group 18: noble gases (e.g., He) — inert
  • Transition metals: groups 3–12 (e.g., Fe) — variable valency
Property Across a period (→) Down a group (↓) Why (simple reason)
Ionization energy Increases Decreases Across: stronger nuclear pull; Down: more shells (shielding)
Atomic/ionic size Decreases Increases Across: more protons pull electrons in; Down: more shells
Electronegativity Increases Decreases Across: stronger pull; Down: electrons farther away
Electron affinity Generally increases Generally decreases Across: attraction increases; Down: attraction weaker

🔗 Chemical Bonding (Why atoms bond)

Atoms bond to achieve a stable noble gas configuration (octet rule, except a few like H/He which aim for 2).

Ionic bond: electron transfer (Na → Na⁺, Cl → Cl⁻)

Bond types (JAMB list)

  • Ionic (electrovalent): electron transfer (metal + nonmetal) → high mp; conducts in solution
  • Covalent: electron sharing (nonmetal + nonmetal); can be polar or nonpolar
  • Hydrogen bond: strong attraction when H is bonded to F/O/N (e.g., water)
  • Metallic bond: “sea” of delocalized electrons → conductivity (e.g., Cu)
  • Dative/coordinate: one atom donates both electrons (complex ions)
  • Van der Waals: weak attractions between molecules
🧠 Fast rule

Large electronegativity difference → likely ionic. Small difference → likely covalent.

📐 Shapes of Simple Molecules (VSEPR)

VSEPR: electron pairs repel, so they arrange themselves to minimize repulsion → determines shape and bond angle.

Common VSEPR shapes: linear, bent, tetrahedral, pyramidal

  • More lone pairs → more repulsion → bond angle becomes smaller (compare CH₄ > NH₃ > H₂O).

📝 3 JAMB-Style Examples

Example 1: Z and A

Question: Z = 11, A = 23. Find protons, neutrons, electrons.

p⁺ = 11

e⁻ = 11 (neutral)

n⁰ = 23 − 11 = 12

Example 2: Isotopes (average mass)

Question: ³⁵Cl (75%), ³⁷Cl (25%). Find RAM.

RAM = (75×35 + 25×37)/100 = (2625 + 925)/100 = 35.5

Note

  • Your draft earlier had 35.25, but the correct arithmetic gives 35.5.

Example 3: Electron configuration + period/group

Question: Z = 20. Write configuration, period, group.

Ca: 1s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s² 3p⁶ 4s²

Period: 4 (highest n = 4)

Group: 2 (valence electrons = 2)
